This pattern, combined with the strong, broad emission at 4561.6 Å, strongly favors identification of this line as Lyα at **z ≈ 2.751**.

The initial Lyα identification at z ≈ 2.751 is strongly supported by the presence of a broad, high-prominence emission line at 4561.566 Å and a dense Lyα forest of narrow absorption lines blueward of this feature. Furthermore, the emission line at 7167.310 Å is in excellent agreement with the expected position of C III] λ1909 at this redshift.

However, this combined solution is physically incoherent, as a single QSO should have a single systemic redshift. The predicted line positions from this average redshift do not align well with the observed strong features.

Crucially, the alternative hypotheses fail to explain the dense forest of narrow absorption lines blueward of 4561 Å. At z ≈ 1.945, the Lyman limit (912 Å) would be observed at ~2687 Å, far outside the band, and the Lyα forest would be between ~2687–3581 Å, a region of very poor data quality where no such forest is discernible. In contrast, the original Lyα hypothesis at z≈2.75 naturally explains this forest as being in the observable range (3500–4561 Å).

Therefore, the evidence strongly **does not support** the hypothesis that the strongest emission line is not Lyα. The original identification of the 4561.566 Å line as Lyα at z ≈ 2.751 remains the most consistent and physically plausible explanation for the entire spectrum.
