Using the Lyα rest wavelength (1216 Å), the corresponding redshift is **z = 2.564**.

Examination of the blue side of this candidate Lyα line (i.e., wavelengths < 4333 Å) reveals several absorption features, including lines at 4237.69 Å, 4068.39 Å, 3980.06 Å, 3759.23 Å, 3656.18 Å, and 3634.10 Å. This clustering and narrowing of absorption lines blueward of the strong emission peak support the identification of the 4333 Å feature as Lyα.

No other detected emission lines correspond to major rest-frame UV/optical features at this redshift.

Step 3: Comprehensive Assessment

The initial identification of the strong emission line at 4333.380 Å as Lyα (z=2.564) is strongly supported by the presence of a Lyα forest and is further confirmed by the detection of C IV λ1549 at 5503.756 Å, which yields a consistent redshift. The line at 6740.380 Å is also a plausible match for C III] λ1909. The redshifts derived from these three lines are:
- Lyα (1216 Å): z = 2.564
- C IV (1549 Å): z = 2.553
- C III] (1909 Å): z = 2.531

Using the flux measurements from the original spectrum (smallest shared sigma) as weights, the weighted average redshift is calculated as **z = 2.553 ± 0.013**.

The emission line at 9471.259 Å was initially considered a candidate for Mg II λ2799. However, at the refined redshift of z=2.553, Mg II is expected at 9944.847 Å, which is significantly redder (~474 Å) than the observed line. This large discrepancy, combined with the low signal-to-noise ratio in that spectral region, makes the identification of the 9471 Å line as Mg II unlikely. It may instead be an unrelated feature or noise.

The confirmed emission lines at the redshift of z = 2.553 are:
